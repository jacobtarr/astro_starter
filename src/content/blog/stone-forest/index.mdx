Tucked away in the southeastern corner of Arizona is a place that feels less like earth and more like a fantasy landscape. Chiricahua National Monument is often called the "Wonderland of Rocks," and it takes only a few minutes on the trail to understand why.

Thousands of bizarre, balancing rock pillars and volcanic hoodoos crowd the canyons, creating a literal forest of stone.

## Walking among the hoodoos

I hiked the Echo Canyon Loop early in the morning, before the heat of the day could reach the canyon floor. The trail winds tightly between massive columns of rhyolite, some of which look so top-heavy you feel as though a strong gust of wind might tip them over.

These formations are the result of a massive volcanic eruption 27 million years ago. Over time, water and ice cracked the cooled ash, and erosion wore away the softer rock, leaving behind these improbable shapes.

Walking through the narrow grottos, surrounded by towering gray-green pillars covered in bright yellow lichen, the rest of the world felt miles away. It is a quiet, labyrinthine sanctuary of stone.
